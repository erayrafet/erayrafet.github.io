The best PWAs on the Internet

Progressive Web Apps is part of Google's effort at unifying the web, so to speak. PWAs are simple websites, under the PWA naming umbrella, with native app like functions. For example, there is the auto dark/light mode based on user' preferences set on the OS level, maskable icons and push notifications. Upcoming features include app shortcuts (Chrome 84-85) like those on Android and running at device startup.
Supported are most browsers based on the Chromium engine, i.e., Chrome, Opera, Vivaldi, Brave, and Microsoft's new Edge as well.

Installing PWAs

To install a PWA, look for a plus (➕) icon on the right-hand side of the address bar on desktop and on mobile you'll see a pop-up at the bottom suggesting you to add an app to the home screen. It's super easy, isn't it?

Suggestions

  • Twitter

    • Without a doubt, the most function-complete progressive app is Twitter. It comes bundled with everything you can expect - even themes.

  • YouTube Music

    • Google's recent transition from Play Music to YouTube Music accelerated the development of the YouTube music web app. They even added an option to upload music files.
    It is now decently performant and pretty much a joy to use, aside from a few quirks here and there.

  • Spotify

    • Spotify is also great at bringing the necessity of features required for a perfect music listening user experience, without requiring you to download multiple apps on multiple computers.

  • Google Photos

    • Google Photos doesn't come short of the mobile Android/iOS apps 'cause interestingly enough, it ships with basic photo editing abilities.

  • Google News

    • Just recently released, Google News is the perfect app for all your news-related entertainment needs. Just give it a go.

  • Lumino PWA

    • Lumino is calculator web app that is boasting a fairly minimal and classy UI.

  • Snapdrop

    • This is very well thought, sleek-looking cross-platform file sharing web app that support mobile as well as desktop.

  • Firefox Send

    • Firefox Send offers similar functionality to Snapdrop, but with a twist. Instead of transferring files only over a local network, everything goes through the Internet with full end-to-end encryption for those of us who are privacy conscious. Maximum file size allowed is 2.5GB.

  • Web Code Editor

    • A free online platform for learning web development on the go, with support for HTML5, CSS, and Javascript.

  • StackEdit

    • StackEdit is an impeccable tool for writing using Markdown, but not only. It stores files locally in browser, so you can continue working whether you are offline or not.

Bonus tip

Well, all Chromium-based browser give you the option of running every website in its own window. Take as an example YouTube. After the recent queue feature rollout, it is suitable at running in a standalone window exactly like the mobile app.
To make that happen, click on the three-dot menu on the right for Chrome and Edge user and select 'Create shortcut'/'Install app'. The same applies to all websites.

My favorite non-standard web apps are Telegram React, Discord, Twitch, Reddit, GitHub, as well as a few others.
